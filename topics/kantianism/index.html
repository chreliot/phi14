Topic 8: Kantianism

German philosopher Immanuel Kant, working in the years before 1800, produced an audacious attempt to ground morality in the idea of the “good will.” We will look at how he does this in the book where he introduces his understanding of morality. Then we consider how we might apply Kantian thinking to particular cases. Finally, we identify criticisms of Kantian reasoning about morality and ways of addressing them.

Reading

  1. “Reasons and Foundations,” chapter 16 of Blackburn, Being Good
  2. “The Categorial Imperative,” chapter 18 of Blackburn, Being Good
  3. Groundwork for the Metaphysic of Morals, by Immanuel Kant (1785), Early Modern Texts version.
    • pages 5–12 (you can finish with the left column of page 12)
    • (an additional selection from this text, pages TBA)
  4. “The Right to Lie: Kant on Dealing with Evil” by Christine M. Korsgaard (1986) Philosophy & Public Affairs 15(4):325–349.

Learning questions

  1. What is the difference between “reasons” and “Reasons” as Blackburn uses those terms?
  2. How does the “good will” potentially offer a foundation for morality?
  3. What is an imperative? What is the difference between a “hypothetical imperative” and a “categorial imperative”?
  4. What do Kant’s three main formulations of the categorical imperative say?
  5. What are examples of three kinds of actions that are (probably) wrong under each of the formulations of the categorical imperative—one action each—and why?
  6. What are some prominent criticisms of Kant’s ethics, and how could one argue, for each of them, that Kant’s ethics gets things wrong?
  7. For each of the criticisms above, how might a Kantian ethicist reply, defending Kantianism?
  8. What exactly is the problem of ’the murderer at the door’ for Kantian ethics.
  9. How does Korsgaard propose to address the problem of the murderer at the door?

Consider

  1. If Kant doesn’t succeed at proving the categorical imperatives are made right by the concept of the good will, why not?
  2. If Kant doesn’t succeed at proving the categorical imperatives are made right by the concept of the good will, how big a problem is that for it? Why?
  3. Having worked through their highlights, does Utilitarianism or Kantianism now seem to you a better approach to ethics/morality than the other? Why?
