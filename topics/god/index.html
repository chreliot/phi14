Topic 2: God

What is ethics if there is no God, or, What would become of ethics if there were no God? If there is a God, does that make all further ethical discussion superfluous?

Reading

  1. “The Death of God” from Being Good, by Simon Blackburn, pages 9–19. (This is the one book you need to purchase for this course.)
  2. Euthyphro (excerpt from Reginald Allen’s translation), by Plato. Available through Hofstra library here, login required. In the left margin of this text are standard section numbers. Before class, you should read sections 2a–8b, pages 41–48.

Additional text for class

  • In class we will also read carefully a few passages of Euthyphro (part of the same text you will have read in advance) that I am not assigning for reading in advance. These are in sections 9c–11b. The section is marked “Second Definition” in the electronic text (direct link here). It’s on pages 49–52. Optionally, of course you may read it—or other parts of the text—in advance!

Learning questions

(These are some of the main things—not all the things—I suggest you learn for this section.)

  1. How might someone support the claim “If God is dead, then everything is permitted?”
  2. If it’s true that God has revealed moral facts to us in the form of divine commands, why might we still need to do philosophical thinking about ethics?
  3. How does Plato argue that even if God(s) command(s) us to do what’s right, that can’t be what makes moral facts true?

Consider

(These more open-ended questions reflect some things I hope this material will have you thinking about for yourself.)

  1. If you participate in a religion or are familiar with a religious system of moral facts, what are they? How complete are they?
  2. If you’re not part of a religion, do you think there is or could be a non-religious source of moral facts?
  3. What room is left for religious instruction if Plato’s main argument in the Euthyphro is right?

See also

  • “Divine Command Metaethics Modified Again,” by Robert Merrihew Adams. 1979. The Journal of Religious Ethics 7(1):66–79. Adams argues here for divine-command theory.
