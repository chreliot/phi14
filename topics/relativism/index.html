Topic 3: Relativism

Reading

  1. “The Challenge of Cultural Relativism” by James Rachels. 1986. from The Elements of Moral Philosophy Temple University Press. Available through the course’s Blackboard site (under Course Documents). Note: this text, like some others we’ll read, includes some outdated language, like “Eskimo”—a word some of those it is used to refer to find unacceptable; I suggest not using it.

Learning questions

  1. What’s the difference between general relativism and moral relativism?
  2. What’s the difference between moral relativism and moral non-realism?
  3. What are Rachels’s two main arguments against (cultural) moral relativism?
  4. Does Rachels demonstrate that moral relativism is false? What exactly does he show?

Consider

  1. Why might someone be attracted to moral relativism? Can you talk it about it in a way that makes it sound true or compelling?
  2. If relativism isn’t the right way to think about the truth-status of moral or ethical rules, what is the right way to think about that?

See also

  • “Cultural Relativism 2.0,” by Michael F. Brown. 2008. Current Anthropology 49(3):363–383.
  • “Moral Relativism” by Chris Gowans in the SEP
  • “Relativism” by Maria Baghramian and J. Adam Carter in the SEP
