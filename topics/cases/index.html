Topic 10: Cases

Having considered the major moral theories of recent philosophical ethics, we think through the moral dimensions of some cases. What do each of the theories have to say about them? Are the theories, as we’ve discussed them, adequate for analyzing these cases?

Reading/viewing

For each of these films, consider making a few notes that prepare you to discuss them.

  1. Watch: Moral Dilemmas … Can Ethics Help? (1998, Intelecom, 28 min.). Freely available on YouTube at the link.

    • This film considers the wrenching situation of a couple newborn babies struggling for life as well as their families and medical staff.
    • The way the participants and narrator frame their moral dilemma might differ from your way of understanding it, including even in language. “Handicapped,” for just one example, is a word that, since 1998, people have widely come to object to. Treat this as an artifact from 1998, and think about whether you agree with its various ways of presenting and analyzing the situation. View critically, as always!
    • You will recognize from our course authors at least two of the philosophers and bioethicists who offer (conflicting) analysis—Martha Nussbaum and James Rachels.

  2. Watch: Enron: The Smartest Guys in the Room (2005, dir. Alex Gibney, 1 h 49 min.) [IMDB listing]

    • You’re probably going to have to pay to stream this one, sorry, unless you somehow have access. It’s currently $0.99 to stream at Apple and Amazon, $2.99 on YouTube, etc. (Hofstra owns the DVD, but not streaming rights.)
    • The film dissects the decline and fall of the seventh largest corporation in the United States at that time.
    • Note the film is rated R, with brief nudity, some swearing, and a dramatized suicide from a distance. I suspect you can handle that, but this maybe isn’t one to watch with your local 10 year-old?

  3. Cases Regional Ethics Bowl Cases: Fall 2020, by Michael Funke, et al., Association for Practical and Professional Ethics (source), and from Prindle Institute for Ethics 2020 Summer High School Ethics Bowl Case Set (source) by Michael Funke, et al. (see Blackboard)

    • “It’s For Your Own Good”
    • “Sisyphus Achievement Awards”
    • “Mugshots in Media”

Learning questions

  1. What do each of the three major moral theories (Utilitarianism, Kantianism, Virtue Ethics) bring out about these non-fiction cases and the various people involved in them?

Consider

  1. How well do the three major moral theories do at bringing out the moral dimensions of these cases? Are there aspects or dimensions they get wrong?
  2. Are there aspects of these situations the moral theories miss—that they fail to reveal the moral significance of? If so, what are they?
