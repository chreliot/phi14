Topic 7: Utilitarianism

In this section, we encounter our first, comprehensive, positive theory of what’s right (and wrong) to do, and why. Utilitarianism, built from the idea that happiness is obviously valuable, resembles a variety of earlier philosophies that do the same, but developed its particular approach from the 18th Century forward. We begin with a slightly later Utilitarian philosopher, John Stuart Mill, who developed a sophisticated defense of it in the 19th Century.

Reading

  1. Utilitarianism by John Stuart Mill, 1863. Read Chapter 2 on your own. Also, short sections of Chapters 1 and 4 will be discussed in class.
    • The above, assigned Early Modern Texts version has been modernized by philosopher Jonathan Bennett from the original, in word choice and sentence structure (Why? How?). You are welcome check out the original, 19th century English version just as Mill wrote it here.
    • Here’s an Epub version if you’d like to read it on a Kindle, tablet, etc.
  2. “Famine, Affluence, and Morality” by Peter Singer, 1972, from Philosophy and Public Affairs 1(3):229–243. (link requires login through Axinn Library; you can download the PDF or read online)

Learning questions

  1. Why might people find it confusing, as Mill discusses, to think that the “useful” could be the same as the “good” and the “right”?
  2. What does “the Greatest Happiness Principle” say, and what does it mean?
  3. How might we apply the Greatest Happiness Principle to any actual situation? What would we need to pay attention to and reason about?
  4. What is the objection to Utilitarianism from the idea of “higher pleasures” as discussed by Mill? (This is sometimes also called “the swine objection.”) How does Mill reply to it?
  5. What is Singer’s main argument for what we should do for others in the world?
  6. Exactly how much should we do for others, if Singer’s analysis is correct—or, more precisely—how should we reason our way to understanding exactly how much we should do?
