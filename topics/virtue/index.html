Topic 9: Virtue

How different does acting well look when we pull back our focus from individual actions, and whether they are right or wrong, to the structure of a whole human life? Acting well can become a matter of what best serves our living the best life we can. So, we have to ask, What’s the best life for a human being? What should we do to aim for that for ourselves?

An influential approach to these questions is that of the Greek philosopher Aristotle, who had been a student of Plato.

This is a slightly longer essay than I’ve assigned before, so you’d do well to pay attention to its structure and try to pick out its main points. It is structured around three main objections to Aristotelian virtue ethics and then Nussbaum’s replies to each of those objections. A successful reading of this text would, at minimum, make note of what each of those are.

Learning questions

  1. How does Aristotle’s conception of happiness (eudaimonia) differ from Mill’s?
  2. What is a virtue, for Aristotle?
  3. What’s an example of an Aristotelian virtue, and why is it virtuous according to him?
  4. Why might Aristotle’s ethics be open to accusations of relativism?
  5. How does Nussbaum defend Aristotelian virtue ethics against accusations of relativism?

Consider

  1. How is Aristotle’s a different approach to the foundations of ethics than Utilitarianism and Kantianism are?
