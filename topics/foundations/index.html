Topic 12: Foundations

We return to the question of the foundations of ethics — indeed to whether ethics has foundations. Blackburn writes “we had to acknowledge the threat of relativism, nihilism, and scepticism.… Are truth and knowledge possible, or does reasoning about what to do eventually hinge on nothing but brute will? Or are there yet other alternatives?” (Being Good, 108).

Reading

  1. “Contracts and Discourse,” chapter 19 of Being Good, by Simon Blackburn
  2. “The Common Point of View,” chapter 20 of Being Good, by Simon Blackburn
  3. “Confidence Restored,” chapter 21 of Being Good, by Simon Blackburn

(about 10 pages total)

Learning questions

  1. What is the idea of a “social contract,” as a foundation for ethics, and what’s one criticism of whether it can effectively provide such a foundation?
  2. What is Blackburn’s idea of “the common point of view,” as a foundation for ethics? How is it supposed to provide such a foundation. And what’s one criticism of whether it can effectively provide such a foundation?
