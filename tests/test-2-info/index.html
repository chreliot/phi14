Test 2 Information

General instructions

The test will be designed take you not more than 90 minutes, but you will have two hours (120 minutes) to complete it. You will need to write answers in pen, on paper. Your answer should be in sentences rather than fragments or bullet points or similar (unless something else is specified by the question).

Format

You will be asked to write answers to six of the questions presented by the test. There will be at least eight options on the test, so that you can skip two. If you answer more than six, I will grade only the first six, so you should choose which questions you think you understand best, and answer those.

The answer to each question should be a minimum of five sentences, and perhaps several more sentences than that, since minimal answers are not always excellent answers.

Content

This test will cover the whole course, but with a strong emphasis on the second half.

How to study

Consider the Learning Questions for each unit, review your notes on the texts, and think through each of the actual test questions, listed below.

Evaluation

Your answers will be assessed in terms of how well they reveal your understanding of the course material. Therefore, answers will need to be constructed as explanations, as if you’re explaining to another student, towards helping them understand. I will try to read them as if I’m a student who is not in the course and think about how well I understand the content based on your answer and explanation.

Actual, possible test-questions

As of this posting, this list is incomplete. (This message will be removed when it is complete.)

  1. What objection to Kantian ethics does Korsgaard address, and how does she attempt to address it?

  2. If you were a Kantian, what might you argue—specifically from a Kantian perspective—is mistaken in Utilitarianism’s foundational assumptions about morality? Then, what’s the argument that it’s mistaken?

  3. If you were to agree with Utilitarianism, what might you argue is wrong with Kantianism’s foundational assumptions morality? And why is it mistaken—what’s the argument?

  4. How might Rule Utilitarianism provide a solution to potential problems with act Utilitarianism? (Specify how they differ.)

  5. What is a virtue, for Aristotle? And is it the same for everyone?

  6. How does Aristotle think we should go about becoming virtuous people? What are two reasons that in practice that might be a quite difficult (or even, perhaps, in some cases, impossible) thing to do?

  7. Why might virtue ethics be susceptible to accusations of relativism? What would be bad about it being relativist?

  8. What is one of Nussbaum’s three concerns about the use of grounding experiences to support the non-relativity of virtue ethics? And how does she answer that concern?

  9. Of Kantianism, Utilitarianism, and Virtue Ethics, which theory is most capable of responding to Glaucon’s challenge in Plato’s Republic, in your view? Why?
