Test 1

This test is designed to take one hour. You may complete it during the class session, or you may submit it later in the day. Tests submitted any time on March 24 will be on time. Submit through the upload link at the course Blackboard site > Tests > Test 1.

Instructions

Choose five (5) of the questions below under “Questions” to work on. For each of those five questions, write a one-paragraph answer, not shorter than five sentences. 6–7 sentences is a good range to aim for, in terms of length (though sentences vary in terms of content, of course, and very brief sentences may not convey as much as I’m assuming in my estimate).

Evaluation

Your answers will be assessed in terms of how well they reveal your understanding of the course material. That includes the clarity of the answer, which includes defining any technical or unfamiliar terms you’re using and writing in clear English.

Source materials

On March 24, from the time class begins, you may not help each other, or communicate with other students about your tests or theirs, or ask for help with the test material from anyone else (besides me, and I won’t give anyone the answers). For the test answers, you may use the assigned readings/texts for the course and your class/reading notes without mentioning them as the sources of any ideas. It is simply assumed that that’s what you’re using. You are discouraged, but not prohibited, from accessing other materials. However, in line with the academic honesty policy for this course described in the syllabus, any incorporation into your answers of ideas or text from non-course materials or other students, without mentioning that you have done so in the form of a citation, will be grounds for earning an F for the course (and the other consequences prescribed by Hofstra Faculty Policy Series 11). More generally, in light of the evaluation criterion above — that I will be assessing each student’s test on how well they reveal that student’s apparent degree of understanding — test answers repeating ideas or text from elsewhere that the test-takers do not seem to understand will not earn good grades.

Questions

  1. What’s a reason to think that human beings are completely selfish? Then, in just 1–2 sentences, what’s a reason to doubt that’s true?

  2. How might someone support, with an argument, the claim “If God is dead, then everything is permitted?”

  3. How does Socrates (in Plato) argue that even if God(s) command(s) us to do what’s right, that can’t be what makes moral facts true?

  4. What are two ways natural selection might threaten ethics?

  5. How might natural selection promote ethics and cooperative behavior?

  6. Why, according to Glaucon (in Plato), might injustice be better than justice? If injustice is better, why — according to him — would people ever behave justly?

  7. Does Rachels demonstrate that moral relativism is false? What exactly does he show?

  8. What is the threat to philosophical ethics from false consciousness? Then, in 1–2 sentences, how does Blackburn counter that it’s not a serious threat?

  9. How might we apply the Greatest Happiness Principle to any actual situation? What would we need to pay attention to and reason about?

  10. What is the objection to Utilitarianism from the idea of “higher pleasures” as discussed by Mill? (This is sometimes also called “the swine objection.”) How does Mill reply to it?

  11. Exactly how much should we do for others, if Singer’s analysis is correct—or, more precisely—how should we reason our way to understanding exactly how much we should do?
