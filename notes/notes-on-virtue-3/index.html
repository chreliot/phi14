Notes on Virtue 3

Criticisms of virtue ethics

  1. How do we get to be virtuous? Aristotle doesn’t offer a recipe. BUT: What about the virtuous mean idea? Aim at the middle. BUT: 50% of the excessive amount of a behavior is normally NOT an amount to aim for. BUT: Also, we tend individually (or collectively) to err on one side or the other, so aiming at the midpoint won’t bring us to the middle way. BUT: There are a lot of formulas/recipes we could bring in (GHP, security-level, decision theory, WWJD) BUT: This just pushes the question of what to do back one level, from What’s the right action? to What’s the right framework/recipe? BUT: We can find viruous people and imitate them. BUT: We don’t know who’s virtuous. And their circumstances might differ. And our society (or our friends, or our families, etc.) might admire the wrong people.

  2. Virtue ethics is relativist. The virtues themselves, and the specific optimizations they involve may reflect the values of an Athenian gentleman. Other cultures may have different virtues. Though Aristotle wants an objective ethics, it may be that the best he can do (or we can do) in presenting a specific set of virtues is to reflect merely what OUR culture values.
