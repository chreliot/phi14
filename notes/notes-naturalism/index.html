Notes on Naturalism

Evolutionary theory

Evolution by natural selection: If organisms have heritable (= can be inherited, passed from parents to offspring) characteristics in which they vary from one another, and some of those variations contribute to their survival-to-reproduce and reproduction), then we will tend to see an increase in those beneficial characteristics over time. Most of the diversity of living things can be explained this way.

Ethics is a behavior. So, can we explain it by natural selection?

Ethics could be a sexually-selected behavior.

Our biological impulses might be self-serving? Does ethics remain without society?

Darwin (1871) Descent of Man: Our early ancestors would have benefitted from banding together in groups. Well-coordinated groups would have tended to survive conflicts with other groups better. Their members would have benefitted also from mutual-aid within the groups. If some group members acquired a heritable tendency toward group-benefitting behavior, that tendency would have tended to spread in the group.

We could also have cultural inheritance as a third mode of selection.

Puzzle: the most self-sacrificing/altruistic people might, for example, die most in wartime. Shouldn’t that undermine the spread of a tendency to self-sacrifice? - Possible answer: Not if if the benefits of altruism outweigh this.

Worry 1: Does evolution make us all selfish?

Answer: The above shows three mechanisms through which altruism could evolve.

Worry 2: If we can explain ethics biologically, any apparently-altruistic behavior might be merely an expression of our biology, and so not real. Ethics/altruism becomes merely a fitness-enhancing behavior, whereas we might want it to be more than that: perhaps a social standard, organizing principle, or genuine, rather than merely automatic, programmed.

Blackburn’s three confusions to guard against

  1. “We can explain how we are.” ≠ “We are different from what we think we are.”

Even if we can explain biologically why mothers normally love babies, it doesn’t follow from that that mothers' love isn’t real. In fact, that we can explain a behavior biologically means it is biologically real. So there’s an error in thinking our kindness, for example, is merely biology and so not real.

  1. Inferring the impossibility of a concern existing from the fact that we don’t have a biological explanation for it.

  2. Reading psychology into nature. Even if natural selection involves a competition among genes, it doesn’t follow that our human moral environment is fundamentally competitive or selfish.

Should selfish agents cooperate?

Game show example: Contestants accumulate big cash rewards. But then, two contestants are set against each other and given the choice of “share or steal.”

  • Both share: each gets half
  • One shares and the other steals: Stealer gets all, sharer gets none
  • Both steal: neither gets any

Is this show a model of any real situations we encounter?

Possibly similar to:

  • taxation? we decide to tax ourselves (by voting for tax-supporting politicians) and that’s like sharing, whereas not taxing and providing services would be like stealing
  • betraying the trust of our collaborators

What kinds of situations have the selfish choice also be the collaborative one?

Evolutionary game theory

Framework for modeling competitive and prosocial behavior.

A “game” has agents, options, and quantified payoffs depicted in matrix.

Prisoner’s Dilemma game

Options:

  • Cooperate (C) - keep trust with colleague
  • Defect (D) - break trust with colleague

Payoff matrix for player 1 (vertical)

   C    D
		 
C  3    1

D  4    2

Strategies for a single game:

  • Focus on risk level: Choose the option with the highest payoff that also has a risk level you’re comfortable with.
  • Focus on maximum benefit: Choose the option with the highest payoff
  • Focus on communal benefit: Choose option where everyone does best
  • Focus on expected value: Choose option with the highest average payoff

Complications of games

  • Iterated game: repeated game, where each player knows how the other played in the past, increasingly. Axelrod discovered the winning strategy for this is “Tit for Tat”: Start by cooperating, but then defect once whenever the other player defects (in the previous game), but then go back to collaborating.
  • Explicit communication among players
  • Alternative payoff matrices

Stag Hunt game

	 Stag  Hare 
Stag  4,4   1,3
Hare  3,1   2,2

where, for X,Y, X is player 1’s payoff and Y is player 2’s payoff

In Stag Hunt games, we do best by cooperating.

What situations in our actual lives, if any, have the structure of one of these games?

  • Two people go into business. This is like Stag Hunt. We do better economically if we work together.
  • Marriage. Shared income/resources, guaranteed companionship. Vs. cheating on the relationship.
  • Trying to be more carbon neutral.
  • Holding onto a friend’s juicy secrets.

We might model these cases as like game-theoretic games, and we can see that

  • even if we assume selfish interest as exclusive motivator, the cooperative solution can be optimal.

The question is: Are actual situations well modeled by games where cooperation does best, or not?
