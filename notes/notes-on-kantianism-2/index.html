Notes on Kantianism 2

Utilitarianism vs. Kantianism

Consider:

  1. Could we actually deploy these theories in our own thinking and acting?
  2. If we did deploy them, would we be behaving well — acting rightly and avoiding wrongs?

Responses

  • It’s difficult to apply either of the moral theories to situations from scratch.

    • BUT: if we routinely apply the theories, they can come to be unconscious habits or have straightforward prescriptions - Kantianism generally yields classes of actions that are right or wrong: using people solely as means is wrong; lying is wrong. So, remembering those things isn’t so complicated! - Utilitarianism: Think at least sometimes whether we can do something positive with broad effects. Consider whether you’re preferring lower to higher pleasures more than occasionally.

  • Utilitarianism might be objectionable as a moral theory, because it tells us to lie, often. And that’s wrong.

    • BUT: While it might sometimes allow lying to produce happiness, overall happiness requires that most of us feel the truth most of the time.

  • Kantianism’s rules are too strict.

    • BUT: Morality is strict.
    • BUT: Kantianism can be reinterpreted to make it less strict and more accurate. (Korsgaard)

  • These theories make different beings morally considerable (= relevant to our moral thinking).

    • Utilitarianism: All the beings capable of pleasure and pain, happiness and unhappiness.
    • Kantianism: All beings with wills (= capable of rationality), though it’s also a good idea not to disrespect other beings, because doing so creates bad habits for us for how we deal with rational beings.

  • Kantianism foregrounds morality, whereas Utilitarianism keeps its eye on happiness (rather than morality per se).
