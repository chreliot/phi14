Notes on Reasons and Foundations

Blackburn distinguishes:

  • reasons (r): motivating considerations for those who care about them
  • Reasons (R): motivation considerations for EVERYONE, independent of their sympathies and inclinations (what they care about and their feelings)

So, are there any “Reasons”? Are there sources of rightness/goodness that should motivate everyone (all rational beings)?

Egoism

Profitability/benefits for me (the person motivated by the reason) are what’s fundamentally good.

  • BUT: This allows only individual goods. So, to the extent that this is the source of goodness, ethics, in the sense of a community establishing what’s right, becomes impossible.

  • BUT: If nobody else can see what’s motivating about what benefits me, then perhaps it’s merely a subjective, not an objective good.

Might makes right

What the stronger say is right is right.

  • BUT: This view will probably not motivating to very many besides the strongest. Can’t be a “Reason.”

Relativism

What’s right is what our culture says is right ( = what’s right is right for a particular culture.)

  • BUT: What’s right is right only within a particular culture, we can’t resolve intercultural conflicts — there’s nothing to appeal to to do that, anyway.

Theistic / divine command

What God wants is what’s good.

  • BUT: We arguably don’t know what God wants, especially about specific cases.
  • BUT: Many people don’t agree that any god exists.
  • BUT: If ethics is to include secular and religious community members, there need to be reasons transcending God’s will. Fortunately, the Euthyphro argument suggests there can be those, even if God exists and promotes what’s right.

Utilitarianism

Happiness is intrinsically good; pain/displeasure are intrinsically bad.

  • BUT: There’s insufficient support for everyone’s happiness always being good.
  • BUT: We disapprove of the happiness of scoundrels.

Kantianism

Good wills are intrinsically good.

  • BUT: What’s good can sometimes result from actions NOT done from good will.
  • BUT: Being rational doesn’t make someone good, worth respecting.
  • BUT: Kant arguably doesn’t have a successful proof of the intrinsic goodness of good will.

(Aristotelian) Virtue Ethics

Eudaimonia is intrinsically good for human beings, or the state in which we’re doing best.

  • BUT: There is no objective state of flourishing for human beings; flourishing is diverse.

Are there alternatives if none of these work?

Social contract theory

Scanlon: an act is wrong if it is rejected by the rules we would agree to, if informed and unforced

This doesn’t tie what’s right to what we actually agree to, but rather to what we WOULD agree to, if informed and unforced and reasonable.

This makes actual or hypothetical agreement the basis of rightness.

Could ethics consist of the principles we (all) actually agree to?

  • BUT: We obviously don’t agree, especially where ethics is relevant.
  • BUT: Any strategy for obtaining actual agreement from everyone is impossible/unworkable.

Could ethics consist of principles that we WOULD hypothetically agree to if given the option to weigh in?

  • BUT: Do different perspectives make it impossible for us to agree on a set of principles?

Seeking a common point of view

Blackburn describes an activity that’s a prerequisite of society:

Let us return to the business of giving and receiving reasons for action, or for attitudes in general/This is an activity that is necessary to us in society. But it is also an activity that seems to require a presupposition. The presupposition is that what I advance as a reason, a reason from my point of view, can be appreciated from your point of view. (129)

Then:

Fortunately we are capable of the common point of view here described. … There is no guarantee that we will come to the same conclusion, of course, but there is a guarantee that we might do so. And that is enough to make the conversation a rational option, better than imposition of one solution on everyone, by force or violence. (130)

So, for Blackburn, behind the common point of view lies a passion, a feeling:

At the back of things there lies a passion: the concern to avoid imposition and manipulation, to be able to reject the charge that their interests have been discounted, and to find just the common standards that enable us to look them in the eye. These may be no more than concerns or passions, but they are all the concerns and passions that enable common humanity to go forward. (131–132)

So, it’s not that ethics is all based just on a feeling. Rather, the continued existence of humanity depends on our being motivated by feelings to participate in the process of finding a common point of view.
